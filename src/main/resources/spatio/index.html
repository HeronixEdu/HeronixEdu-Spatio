📏 Measurements & Units ▼

Why Do Units Matter?

When you 3D print something, the printer needs to know exactly how big to make it. A cube that's 2 cm wide is about the size of a dice. A cube that's 2 inches wide is about the size of a marshmallow!

Common Units

mm (millimeter) — Width of a pencil tip. 10 mm = 1 cm
cm (centimeter) — Width of your pinky finger. 100 cm = 1 meter
inch (in) — About 2.5 cm. Width of a quarter coin
foot (ft) — 12 inches = 30.48 cm. About the length of a ruler

Converting Between Units

1 inch = 25.4 mm = 2.54 cm
1 cm = 10 mm = 0.394 inches
1 foot = 12 inches = 30.48 cm
1 meter = 100 cm = 3.28 feet
Use the Measure tab to see live dimensions of your selected shape in different units!

Fractions of an Inch

In the US, measurements often use fractions instead of decimals:

½ inch = 0.5 in = 12.7 mm
¼ inch = 0.25 in = 6.35 mm
⅛ inch = 0.125 in = 3.175 mm
1/16 inch = 0.0625 in = 1.588 mm

Real-World Scale Reference

📱 Phone: ~15 cm × 7 cm × 0.8 cm
⚽ Soccer ball: ~22 cm diameter
📓 Textbook: ~28 cm × 22 cm × 2 cm
🎲 Dice: ~1.5 cm × 1.5 cm × 1.5 cm
🏠 Door: ~200 cm × 80 cm × 4 cm
🧮 Geometry Formulas ▼

Volume (how much space inside)

🟦 Box: V = length × width × height
🔵 Sphere: V = ⁴⁄₃ × π × r³
🫙 Cylinder: V = π × r² × h
🔺 Cone: V = ⅓ × π × r² × h
🔷 Pyramid: V = ⅓ × base area × h
🍩 Torus: V = 2π² × R × r²

Surface Area (outside skin)

🟦 Box: SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
🔵 Sphere: SA = 4 × π × r²
🫙 Cylinder: SA = 2πr(r + h)
🔺 Cone: SA = πr(r + slant)
Select any shape and check the Measure tab to see these formulas calculated live!

Key Math Symbols

π (pi) = 3.14159... — the magic circle number!
r = radius (distance from center to edge)
r² = r squared = r × r
r³ = r cubed = r × r × r
h = height

Perimeter & Circumference

Square: P = 4 × side
Rectangle: P = 2(l + w)
Circle: C = 2 × π × r = π × d
Triangle: P = a + b + c
📐 Angles & Rotation ▼

What Are Degrees?

A full spin = 360°. Half turn = 180°. Quarter turn = 90°. These numbers show up everywhere in 3D!

Common Angles

0° — No rotation (facing forward)
45° — Half of a right angle (diagonal)
90° — Right angle (📐 corner of a book)
180° — Half turn (facing backward)
270° — Three-quarter turn
360° — Full rotation (back to start!)

Rotation Axes (how things spin)

X axis Y axis Z axis

X rotation = tilt forward/backward (like nodding)

Y rotation = spin left/right (like shaking head "no")

Z rotation = roll sideways (like tilting head)

In Spatio, rotation snaps to 15° by default. Hold Shift for finer 5° rotation!

Symmetry

Symmetry = when both halves match. Use the Mirror/Flip buttons to test symmetry!

🔵 Sphere — infinite lines of symmetry
🟦 Cube — 9 planes of symmetry
⭐ 5-point star — 5 lines of symmetry
❤️ Heart — 1 line of symmetry
🔍 Scale & Proportion ▼

What Is Scale?

Scale means making something bigger or smaller while keeping the same shape. A model car is a "scaled down" version of a real car!

Scale Factors

2x scale = double the size (8x the volume!)
0.5x scale = half the size (⅛ the volume)
1x scale = same size (no change)
When you double all dimensions (2x), the volume becomes 2 × 2 × 2 = 8 times bigger! That's why elephants need such thick legs.

Uniform vs Non-Uniform Scale

Uniform: Scale X, Y, Z equally — keeps proportions

Non-uniform: Scale each axis differently — stretches or squishes

Aspect Ratio

The relationship between width and height. A square has 1:1 ratio. A widescreen TV is 16:9. A phone is about 9:19.

☕ Volume in Action: The Cup Challenge ▼

🏫 Teacher Activity: How Much Water Fits?

Have students model a cup in Spatio Studio, then calculate how much water it can hold!

Step 1: Model a Cup

A simple cup is a cylinder. A real coffee mug is about:

Diameter: 8 cm (radius = 4 cm)
Height: 10 cm

Step 2: Calculate the Volume

V = π × r² × h
V = 3.14159 × 4² × 10
V = 3.14159 × 16 × 10
V = 502.65 cm³

Step 3: Convert to Milliliters

1 cm³ = 1 mL (milliliter). So our cup holds about 503 mL — that's about 2 cups of water!

💡 Class Discussion

  • What happens if you make the cup taller but thinner? Does it hold more or less?
  • How wide would a cup need to be to hold exactly 1 liter (1000 mL)?
  • A bathtub holds ~300 liters. How many cups is that?

Challenge: The Cone Cup

Paper cups from water coolers are cone-shaped! A cone holds ⅓ the volume of a cylinder with the same radius and height.

Vcone = ⅓ × π × r² × h
Vcone = ⅓ × 502.65
Vcone = 167.55 mL — much less!

Real-World Connection

☕ Coffee mug ≈ 350 mL (12 oz)
🥤 Soda can ≈ 355 mL (12 oz)
🥛 Milk carton ≈ 236 mL (8 oz = 1 cup)
🏊 Olympic pool ≈ 2,500,000 liters!
🎁 Surface Area: Wrapping Paper Challenge ▼

🏫 Teacher Activity: How Much Wrapping Paper?

Students model a gift box and calculate how much wrapping paper they need!

The Gift Box

A shoebox is about: 30 cm × 18 cm × 10 cm

Calculate Surface Area

SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
SA = 2(30×18 + 30×10 + 18×10)
SA = 2(540 + 300 + 180)
SA = 2 × 1020
SA = 2,040 cm² of wrapping paper
That's about 0.2 m². A standard roll of wrapping paper is 5 m², so you could wrap about 25 shoeboxes with one roll!

Wrapping a Ball (Sphere)

Wrapping a basketball (diameter 24 cm, radius 12 cm):

SA = 4 × π × r²
SA = 4 × 3.14159 × 12²
SA = 1,809.6 cm²

That's actually less than the shoebox! But try wrapping a ball smoothly... 😅

🏗️ Real-World: Painting a Room

Painters calculate surface area to know how much paint to buy:

Room: 4m × 5m × 2.5m tall
Wall area: 2(4×2.5 + 5×2.5) = 45 m²
1 gallon of paint covers ~35 m²
You'd need about 1.3 gallons (buy 2!)
📐 Pythagorean Theorem in 3D ▼

The Most Famous Formula in Math!

a² + b² = c²

In any right triangle, the square of the longest side (hypotenuse) equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

🏫 Teacher Activity: The Ramp

Students build a ramp (wedge shape) and calculate its slope length!

Base (a): 4 cm (ground length)
Height (b): 3 cm (how tall)
Slope (c): ? cm (the ramp surface)
c² = a² + b²
c² = 4² + 3²
c² = 16 + 9 = 25
c = √25
c = 5 cm — The famous 3-4-5 triangle!

Pythagorean Triples (whole number answers)

3, 4, 5 — The classic! (3²+4² = 9+16 = 25 = 5²)
5, 12, 13 — (25+144 = 169 = 13²)
8, 15, 17 — (64+225 = 289 = 17²)
7, 24, 25 — (49+576 = 625 = 25²)

3D Diagonal — Pythagorean Theorem Extended!

To find the diagonal through a box (corner to opposite corner):

d = √(l² + w² + h²)

Example: A box that's 3 × 4 × 12 cm:

d = √(9 + 16 + 144) = √169
d = 13 cm
Build a 3×4×12 box in Spatio and measure it! The diagonal from one corner to the opposite corner is exactly 13 cm.

🏫 Classroom Challenges

  • TV Size: A 55" TV is measured diagonally. If it's 48" wide, how tall is the screen? (Answer: √(55² - 48²) = √721 ≈ 26.8")
  • Ladder Safety: A 5m ladder leans against a wall, with its base 3m from the wall. How high does it reach? (Answer: 4m)
  • Baseball Diamond: The bases are 90 feet apart. How far is it from home plate to second base? (Answer: 90√2 ≈ 127.3 ft)
🖨️ Math Meets 3D Printing ▼

🏫 Teacher Activity: Print Cost Calculator

How much does it cost to 3D print something? It depends on volume!

Step 1: Calculate Volume

Let's say you designed a cube pencil holder: 8 × 8 × 10 cm with 15% infill.

Total volume = 8 × 8 × 10 = 640 cm³
Actual plastic (15% infill) = 640 × 0.15
Plastic used ≈ 96 cm³

Step 2: Convert to Weight

PLA density = 1.24 g/cm³
Weight = 96 × 1.24 = 119 grams

Step 3: Calculate Cost

PLA filament: ~$20 per 1 kg spool
Cost = 119g × ($20 / 1000g)
Cost ≈ $2.38 to print!
Most classroom prints cost between $0.50 and $5.00. The biggest cost factor is size — double the dimensions = 8x the material!

Print Time Estimation

Average speed: ~50 mm/second
Our pencil holder: ~2.5 hours
A phone case: ~1.5 hours
A chess piece: ~45 minutes

  • Science: Material properties, density, melting points
  • Art: Design, proportion, aesthetics
  • Engineering: Structural integrity, support design, tolerances
  • Economics: Cost analysis, material efficiency, production planning
🌐 Spatial Concepts ▼

What Is 3D Space?

We live in a 3-dimensional world! Every location has three coordinates — like a secret address in space.

X axis Y axis Z axis

X = Left ↔ Right   Y = Up ↕ Down   Z = Forward ↔ Back

Coordinates

Every point is written as (X, Y, Z). The center is (0, 0, 0) — called the Origin. The grid you see is centered there!

Transformations

  • Translation — Moving an object
  • Rotation — Spinning around an axis
  • Scale — Making bigger or smaller
Place a cube at (3, 0, 3). That's 3 right and 3 forward from center!

Perspective & Views

Orbit around your scene (drag) to see it from all angles. Real designers check Front, Side, and Top views to make sure everything looks right!

Object Origin

Each object has its own origin — the center point it rotates and scales around. That small point is like the object's "belly button"!

📐 Geometry Basics ▼

Building Blocks of 3D Shapes

  • Vertex — A single point (a corner)
  • Edge — A line connecting two vertices
  • Face — A flat surface bounded by edges
  • Mesh — All the faces making a 3D shape

Shape Facts

🟦 Cube — 8 vertices, 12 edges, 6 faces
🔺 Pyramid — 5 vertices, 8 edges, 5 faces
🫙 Cylinder — 2 circular faces + 1 curved face
🔵 Sphere — ~500+ triangular faces!

Why Triangles?

All 3D models are made of triangles (called polygons). Triangles are always flat — you can't make a wonky triangle! Computers love them.

A sphere on a computer isn't really round — it's hundreds of tiny triangles shaped to look round!

Volume & Surface Area

Volume = how much space a shape fills (like water in a cup). Surface Area = the total area of all faces added together.

Cube formula: Volume = side³  |  Surface = 6 × side²

Symmetry

A shape has symmetry when you can cut it in half and both sides match. A sphere has infinite symmetry. A cube has 9 planes of symmetry!

Convex vs Concave

A convex shape has no dents (like a ball). A concave shape dents inward (like a bowl). Stars are concave!

🖨️ 3D Printing Guide ▼

How Does 3D Printing Work?

A 3D printer builds objects layer by layer from the bottom up — like stacking thousands of paper-thin sheets to build a tower!

Types of 3D Printers

  • FDM — Melts plastic into thin strands. Most common & beginner-friendly!
  • Resin (SLA) — UV light hardens liquid resin. Very detailed but messy.
  • SLS — Laser fuses powder together. Used in factories.

Common Materials

  • PLA ⭐ — Easy to print, made from plants! Great for beginners
  • PETG — Stronger & a bit flexible. Good for functional parts
  • ABS — Very tough but tricky. Used in car parts
  • TPU — Flexible like rubber. Perfect for phone cases!

Key Settings in a Slicer

  • Layer Height — Thinner = smoother but slower (0.1–0.3 mm)
  • Infill % — How solid the inside is. 15% = mostly hollow. 100% = solid
  • Supports — Temporary columns holding up steep overhangs
  • Print Speed — Slower = better quality
Overhangs steeper than 45° usually need supports. Design flat-bottomed models to skip them and save time!

From Screen to Reality

Step 1 — Design in Spatio Studio and export STL

Step 2 — Open STL in a Slicer (Cura, PrusaSlicer, Bambu)

Step 3 — Slicer creates G-code instructions for the printer

Step 4 — Printer builds it layer by layer 🎉

Design Tips for 3D Printing

  • Flat surfaces print best — avoid unsupported ceilings
  • Minimum wall thickness: 1.2 mm (2× nozzle width)
  • Make sure your model has NO holes (watertight)
  • Add a flat base so your model sticks to the print bed
  • Export as .STL for most printers

Troubleshooting Tips

  • Print not sticking? Clean the bed & add a brim
  • Stringy between parts? Lower temperature or increase retraction
  • Layer separation? Increase temperature or slow down
  • Warping? Use an enclosure or heated bed
